PAGES 3-4 — HOW TO USE THIS BOOK (2 pages)
(Copy and paste into Affinity Publisher)
-------------------------------------
Welcome to The Wine Tasting Journal Notebook — the no-fuss way to
remember every wine you try.

This journal is made for people who love wine but don't want to spend
twenty minutes writing tasting notes every time they open a bottle. It's
fast, simple, and designed to fit into the way you actually drink wine —
whether that's a casual Tuesday pour at home, a glass at a restaurant, or
a Saturday afternoon at a friend's dinner party.

There is no right or wrong way to use it. The goal is just to capture
enough that you'll remember the wine later.

HOW THE TASTING LOG WORKS (pages 5-104)

Each page holds two quick entries. For every wine you try, just fill in:

WINE — Write the name of the wine. Even a partial name or a rough
description works. Something like "the French red from Whole Foods" is
perfectly fine.

DATE — When you tried it. Handy for remembering seasons or occasions.

TYPE — Tick the box for red, white, rose, or sparkling. One tap and
you're done.

MY RATING — Mark as many of the five boxes as the wine deserves. One box
means it wasn't great. Five boxes means buy more immediately.

NOTES — This is your space. Write whatever comes to mind — the smell, the
taste, the food you had with it, who you shared it with, or just whether
you'd buy it again. There are no rules here. Even one sentence is better
than nothing.

That's it. Two entries per page, no jargon required.

HOW THE "WINES TO BUY" LIST WORKS (pages 105-114)

These pages are your personal wine shopping list. When someone recommends
a wine, when you see something interesting on a menu, or when you finish
a bottle and want to remember to get it again — write it here.

Each row has:
- A checkbox to tick when you've bought (or found) the wine
- Space for the wine name
- A column for where to find it — a shop, a restaurant, a website
- A column for the price so you can budget ahead

Use these pages as your running list between shopping trips. Cross off or
tick the box when you've got it, and start a new row for the next one.

A tip: keep this notebook with you. The best time to log a wine is right
when you're drinking it — before the details fade. Pull it out at the
table, fill in the quick fields, and you've done it. Your future self
will thank you.
