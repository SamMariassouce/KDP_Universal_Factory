How to Use This Journal

You are holding a serious analytical tool — designed for tasters who want
to go beyond simply enjoying wine and start building a deep, documented
understanding of what is in the glass. Whether you are working toward a
WSET or Court of Master Sommeliers qualification, following the methodology
of Wine Folly, or simply committed to developing a more refined palate,
this journal gives you the framework to do exactly that.

This journal contains three distinct template types, each serving a
specific function in your tasting workflow. Here is how to use them together:

COLOR AND CLARITY ANALYSIS PAGES

Use these pages first, before you take a single sip. Visual assessment is
the starting point of every structured tasting, and training your eye to
read a wine accurately is a skill that develops with consistent practice.
Fill in the color depth, hue, clarity, brilliance, and viscosity for each
wine. Use the sketch box to draw or shade in the actual color you observe
in the glass. Over time, these pages will train you to spot signs of age,
oxidation, and variety character just from appearance alone.

DETAILED FLAVOR MAPPING PAGES

These are the heart of the journal. Use one page per wine, working
methodically through each section. Do not skip sections even when a
particular attribute is subtle — the absence of something (low tannin,
no oak influence, short finish) is as important as its presence. When
scoring the palate structure scales, mark your position on the sliding
scale with an X or a vertical line. For the flavor mapping text area,
write your impressions in plain language. You do not need to identify
every note perfectly — the goal is to train your descriptive memory so
that next time you taste this wine, or a wine like it, you recognize what
you are sensing and why.

Score each wine on the 1-100 point scale used by major critics. This is
not about matching others' scores — it is about building a personal
reference library of how a given wine performs for you on a given day.

VINTAGE SUCCESS CHARTS BY REGION

Use these pages as a running reference document. After tasting wines from
a particular region and vintage, record your overall assessment of that
vintage using the 1-5 scale (1 = Poor, 5 = Outstanding). Over time, your
charts will reflect your own tasting data — not just published vintage
reports. This is particularly valuable for identifying personal preference
patterns: you may discover that you consistently enjoy certain regions
in cooler vintages, or that your palate favors early-release years over
wines that require long cellaring.

Getting the Most Out of This Journal

Taste systematically. Whenever possible, taste blind or at least taste
multiple wines side by side rather than in isolation. Comparative tasting
accelerates learning faster than any other approach.

Review your old entries regularly. Go back to pages you completed 3, 6,
or 12 months ago and compare them to your current tasting notes for the
same wine. Your descriptive vocabulary and analytical precision will
improve noticeably, and you will spot patterns you could not see before.

Do not be afraid of being wrong. If you note a specific aroma and later
discover the bottle shows something entirely different, that is useful
data. Log it. Your errors are as instructive as your accurate calls.

This journal belongs to you. No entry is too small, too uncertain, or too
informal. Every note you make here is a data point in building the most
important wine reference you will ever own — the one calibrated entirely
to your own palate.
