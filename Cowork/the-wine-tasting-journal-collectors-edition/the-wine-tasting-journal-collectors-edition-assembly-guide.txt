PAGES 3-4 — HOW TO USE THIS BOOK (2 pages)
(Copy and paste into Affinity Publisher)
-------------------------------------
Welcome to The Wine Tasting Journal: Collector's Edition — a permanent
reference built for the serious wine collector. This is not a casual
tasting diary. It is a professional archive designed to document your
cellar, track the technical merits of the wines you evaluate, and record
the investment value of the bottles you acquire. Over time, this journal
becomes one of the most valuable documents in your collection.

This journal contains three distinct sections, each with a specific
purpose. Here is how to use each one.

CELLAR INVENTORY AND AGING TRACKER (pages 5-34)

Use these pages to record every bottle or case you bring into your cellar.
Each entry captures the essential details of a wine's identity — producer,
vintage, appellation, and grape variety — alongside your acquisition
details, including purchase date, price, and vendor. The aging fields
allow you to record your optimal drinking window, your projected peak
year, and your storage location so you can find any bottle at a glance.

The aging notes section is where you record the winemaking profile of the
bottle: its expected development arc, its tannin structure at the time of
purchase, and any expert opinions or critic scores that informed your
buying decision. At the bottom, the tasting history table gives you space
to log brief impressions each time you open a bottle from the case — so
you can track how the wine evolves across multiple openings over the years.

Reserve one inventory page per wine or per case. If you have multiple
vintages of the same producer, give each its own page.

ADVANCED TECHNICAL TASTING SHEETS (pages 35-184)

These pages are the analytical core of the journal. Each sheet is built
around a systematic approach to wine evaluation: Appearance, Nose, Palate,
and Conclusions. You are guided through every dimension of the wine — from
colour intensity and clarity, through primary, secondary, and tertiary
aromas, to a five-point scale assessment of sweetness, acidity, tannin,
body, and alcohol. The conclusions section records a quality rating,
drinking readiness, an optimal window, and a score.

Use one tasting sheet per wine per occasion. If you taste the same wine
across multiple sittings or vintages, give each its own sheet. These
sheets are ideal for formal tastings, cellar evaluations, competitive
blind tastings, and structured comparative flights.

Be as precise as you can in your descriptors. The more specific your
language, the more useful this record will be when you return to it in
five or ten years.

WINE INVESTMENT AND VALUE LOGS (pages 185-204)

These pages are designed for collectors who approach fine wine as an
asset. Each log captures the full financial profile of a position in your
cellar: purchase details, insurance valuation, current bottles remaining,
and a five-row market value history table so you can track appreciation
over time. The disposition section records what happened to the wine
when you eventually sold it, drank it, or gifted it — and whether you
made or lost money on the position.

Use one investment log per wine position or per case purchase. Update
the market value history table annually or whenever you receive a new
valuation from your merchant or auction house.

A few notes for best results:

Write in pen, not pencil. This is a permanent record. Use consistent
date formatting across all entries — for example, always write dates
as DD/MM/YYYY or YYYY-MM-DD. Cross-reference your tasting sheets to
your cellar inventory pages by noting the page number of each tasting
entry in the tasting history table. Over time this journal becomes
a searchable reference for every wine you have ever owned or evaluated.
